# Writing & Composition (the bridge)

**Strand of [Kaelyn's Adaptive Curriculum](../README.md)** · See the master design, especially **§4 "The writing bridge"** — this strand is that idea made into rungs.

Kaelyn reads chapter books and multiplies, but her writing is at **words and labels** (single words, very short phrases). That gap is not a weakness; it is **asynchronous development**. Her ideas are years ahead of her hand. If we make "writing" mean "form every letter neatly," we trap a chapter-book mind inside single words and quietly teach her that writing is the thing she is bad at. We will not do that.

So this strand does the one thing §4 insists on: it **separates two jobs** and runs them on **two separate ladders** that climb at their own speeds.

- **Job A — Composition (her ideas → language).** This runs at her *thinking* level starting today. She composes out loud and an adult **scribes** it (types or writes it down), or she **dictates** it (speech-to-text), or she **types** it herself. Sentence frames and word banks carry the wording so the transcription load stays near zero. She arranges and revises real pieces. This is where her true ability shows, and it is the ladder that matters most.
- **Job B — Transcription (handwriting + mechanics).** A **short, gentle, separate** daily track: letter formation, spacing, capitals and periods, a little stamina at a time. Small doses. It climbs its own ladder and is **never the gate on her ideas.** As her hand catches up, more of Job A naturally shifts to handwritten — but composition never waits for it.

**The promise we keep:** she hears "**you're an author**" long before she hears "neat handwriting." We celebrate ideas first, every time. We scribe generously. We keep handwriting kind and brief. We never, ever push writing to frustration. A six-year-old who decides she "hates writing" is a wound that takes years to heal, and the whole point of this strand is to make sure that never happens.

**How to read the rungs.** Every composition rung names a **mode** — *scribed / dictated / typed / handwritten* — so it is always clear that her hand is not what is being tested. Early rungs are **scribed-or-typed**; **handwriting enters only as Job B allows**, and only ever for short, already-composed pieces. The **composition mastery gate is about ideas, sentence structure, and organization — not handwriting.** Handwriting has its own gate on the Job B ladder, and the two are scored separately.

> **Mastery gate (default, from §2):** a skill is "solid" when she succeeds **independently on ≥ 4 of 5 fresh items across ≥ 2 different days** (retention, not a lucky session). Below that she is "emerging" and gets more, varied practice. For composition, "independently" means *she supplies the ideas and structure herself* — being scribed for is still independent composition. Stretch is always offered, never forced.

Most composition activities are an extended **`journal-prompt`** interaction (the "compose" activity type that carries the bridge) with the mode set to **scribe**, **dictate**, or **type**. A drag-to-order **arrange** interaction and a **`project-checklist`** turn for revision round it out. Transcription uses a light **trace/copy** interaction, kept to a few minutes.

## Ladder A — Composition (Job A: her ideas → language)

> The mode is named on every rung. It tells us *who carries the transcription*, never *whether the idea counts*. Her idea always counts.

### R-WC-1 · Label & caption (word → phrase)
**Can-do:** She labels a picture or object with a single accurate word, then stretches it to a short phrase that adds one detail ("volcano" → "hot volcano" → "the tall volcano").
**Mode:** *scribed or typed* (she says or types the word/phrase; handwriting not required).
**Sample activities (expedition-flavored):**
- *Label the diagram* (`journal-prompt`, scribe/type): on a volcano cross-section she names the parts she knows ("magma," "ash," "crater"); the tutor offers a small word bank as support, not a script.
- *Caption the photo* (`journal-prompt`, scribe/type): a whale photo; she gives a two-or-three-word caption ("a huge whale," "whale jumping high").
- *Word-bank sort-and-stick:* she picks the best word for each picture from a themed bank, then adds one describing word.
**Mastery gate:** independently provides accurate labels and stretches at least one into a 2–3 word phrase, on ≥ 4 of 5 items across ≥ 2 days. (Spelling and letter shapes are not scored here.)
**Stretch:** turn a caption into a whole spoken sentence — which is exactly R-WC-2.

### R-WC-2 · One complete sentence (oral → scribed, then with a frame)
**Can-do:** She says one complete sentence (a who/what that makes sense and feels finished) and sees it captured as text; then she completes a sentence frame to make her own.
**Mode:** *oral → scribed*, then *scribed or typed with a frame* (her hand carries none of it yet).
**Sample activities:**
- *Say it, see it* (`journal-prompt`, scribe): she tells one true sentence about today's expedition ("Whales are the biggest animals in the ocean."); the adult scribes it and reads it back so she watches her idea become text.
- *Finish the frame* (`journal-prompt`, scribe/type): "The volcano erupted because ______." / "I learned that ______." She supplies the idea; the frame carries the structure.
- *Sentence vs. not-a-sentence:* the tutor shows a phrase ("the red lava") and a sentence ("The red lava flowed downhill."); she picks which one is finished and fixes the other out loud.
**Mastery gate:** independently produces a complete, sensible sentence (orally-then-scribed or via a frame) on ≥ 4 of 5 prompts across ≥ 2 days. The judge is *completeness and meaning*, not transcription.
**Stretch:** add a "where" or "when" to her sentence — the doorway to R-WC-3.

### R-WC-3 · Expand a sentence (add detail, stronger words)
**Can-do:** She grows a bare sentence by adding who / what / when / where / why, swapping in an adjective, or choosing a stronger verb ("The dog ran." → "The scared puppy ran fast across the yard.").
**Mode:** *scribed or typed* (revision is spoken or dragged; no handwriting tax on her thinking).
**Sample activities:**
- *Grow the sentence* (`journal-prompt`, scribe/type): the tutor gives a plain sentence; she adds one expansion at a time (an adjective, then a "where," then a "why"), watching it get richer.
- *Stronger-verb swap:* "The boat went on the water" → she trades "went" for "raced," "drifted," or "crashed," and says how the meaning changes.
- *Five-W detective* (`journal-prompt`, dictate): given "The pharaoh built it," she dictates the missing who/what/when/where/why to make it vivid.
**Mastery gate:** independently expands a given sentence with ≥ 2 meaningful additions (detail, adjective, or stronger verb) on ≥ 4 of 5 items across ≥ 2 days.
**Stretch:** write a second sentence that connects to the first — that is R-WC-4.

### R-WC-4 · Two related sentences
**Can-do:** She composes two sentences that belong together — a statement and a detail, or a cause and an effect — using a simple connector ("and," "but," "so," "because") when it fits.
**Mode:** *scribed or typed* (typing is offered here so length never costs her a struggle).
**Sample activities:**
- *And-then / because* (`journal-prompt`, scribe/type): "Volcanoes are dangerous. People move away because hot lava can burn their homes." She supplies both ideas and the link.
- *Fact + so-what* (`journal-prompt`, type): one fact about whales, then one sentence about why it's amazing.
- *Glue the pair:* the tutor gives two short sentences; she joins or reorders them so they flow, then reads the smoother version aloud.
**Mastery gate:** independently composes two sentences that are clearly related (and uses a connector appropriately at least sometimes) on ≥ 4 of 5 prompts across ≥ 2 days.
**Stretch:** add a third sentence so it has a beginning, a middle, and an end — that is R-WC-5.

### R-WC-5 · Three-sentence pieces across all three genres
**Can-do:** She composes a complete little piece with a beginning, middle, and end, and she can do it in **three genres**: a **narrative** (something happened), an **informational** piece (2–3 facts on a topic), and an **opinion** ("I think ______ because ______.").
**Mode:** *scribed, dictated, or typed* (her choice for the day; length is fine because her hand isn't carrying it).
**Sample activities:**
- *Tiny story* (`journal-prompt`, dictate/scribe — narrative): "First the ground shook. Then the volcano erupted. Everyone ran to safety." Beginning, middle, end in three sentences.
- *Three true facts* (`journal-prompt`, type — informational): three facts about Ancient Egypt she actually knows, in an order that makes sense.
- *I think… because* (`journal-prompt`, scribe/dictate — opinion): "I think whales are the best ocean animal because they are huge, they sing, and they are gentle."
- *Genre detective:* the tutor reads three short pieces; she names which is a story, which teaches facts, and which gives an opinion — then composes a matching one.
**Mastery gate:** independently composes a coherent 3-sentence piece with clear beginning/middle/end in **each** of the three genres (narrative, informational, opinion), ≥ 4 of 5 across ≥ 2 days per genre. The judge is structure and sense, never neatness.
**Stretch:** add a detail sentence to the middle so it's growing toward a paragraph — that is R-WC-6.

### R-WC-6 · A short paragraph (topic sentence + details)
**Can-do:** She composes a short paragraph that opens with a topic sentence, follows with 2–3 supporting detail sentences, and stays on one idea.
**Mode:** *typed or dictated* (typing is the default here — a keyboard sidesteps fine-motor entirely so she can write a *paragraph about whales while her hand is still mastering lowercase b*).
**Sample activities:**
- *Topic + three details* (`journal-prompt`, type/dictate): "Whales are amazing ocean animals." + three sentences of supporting detail, all about whales.
- *Stay-on-topic check:* the tutor mixes one off-topic sentence into a paragraph; she finds and removes the one that doesn't belong, and says why.
- *Expedition mini-report* (`journal-prompt`, type): a short paragraph answering an expedition "why" question (Why do volcanoes erupt?), opening with her topic sentence.
**Mastery gate:** independently composes a paragraph with a clear topic sentence and ≥ 2 on-topic supporting details, ≥ 4 of 5 across ≥ 2 days. Organization and focus are the gate — not handwriting, not spelling.
**Stretch:** reread her own paragraph and improve one word or reorder one sentence — that is R-WC-7.

### R-WC-7 · Revise & edit (word choice, order, add detail)
**Can-do:** She rereads her own piece and makes it better on purpose: swaps a weak word for a stronger one, reorders sentences so they flow, adds a missing detail, and (lightly) checks that sentences start with a capital and end with a mark.
**Mode:** *typed or scribed* — revision is the point, so it happens where editing is frictionless (drag to reorder, tap to swap a word); mechanics editing connects to Job B but is never the focus here.
**Sample activities:**
- *Make it stronger* (`project-checklist` + `journal-prompt`, type/scribe): a 3-item reviser's checklist ("a stronger word? a clearer order? one more detail?"); she applies each to her own draft.
- *Drag to order* (arrange interaction): her own (or a scrambled model's) sentences as cards; she drags them into the order that flows best and reads the result aloud.
- *Word upgrade:* the tutor highlights a bland word in her draft ("good," "big," "went"); she picks a livelier one and keeps the meaning.
**Mastery gate:** independently makes ≥ 2 meaningful revisions (word choice, order, or added detail) to her own piece and can say *why* each is better, across ≥ 2 days. Capitals/periods are noticed, but composition mastery is judged on the *ideas and organization*.
**Stretch:** take a favorite revised piece to "published" — pick a title, add a drawing, and read it aloud as the author. Then reach into a longer or new-genre piece the tutor offers.

## Ladder B — Transcription (Job B: handwriting + mechanics)

> This ladder is **short, gentle, and separate.** Small daily doses (a few minutes inside the Write block). It is **never the gate on her composition.** Its only job is to let her hand slowly catch up to her mind, with zero pressure. If a session feels frustrating, we stop and scribe — the ideas already have a home on Ladder A.

### R-WT-1 · Letter formation by stroke groups
**Can-do:** She forms letters with correct, consistent strokes, learned in **stroke-family groups** rather than alphabetically — first big top-to-bottom and left-to-right lines and circles, then the families that build on them (l, t, i / c, o, a, d / m, n, r, h / v, w, x, y), lowercase first.
**Mode:** *handwritten* (trace → copy → form from memory), a few letters at a time.
**Sample activities (kept light and playful):**
- *Stroke-family of the day* (trace/copy): trace then copy 2–3 letters from one family, with a verbal cue ("pull down, then over").
- *Sky-write & sand-write:* she writes the day's letters big in the air or in a tray first, building the motor pattern before pencil-and-paper.
- *Letter hunt in her own words:* find today's letter inside a word she composed on Ladder A, so handwriting connects to her real ideas.
**Mastery gate:** forms the letters in the current stroke family legibly and with correct stroke direction on most attempts, across ≥ 2 days. Legible, not perfect.
**Stretch:** try the next stroke family, or write a short word she chooses using letters she's mastered.

### R-WT-2 · Consistent spacing & sizing
**Can-do:** She leaves a clear, consistent space between words and keeps letters roughly the same size, sitting on the line.
**Mode:** *handwritten*, short bursts (a phrase or one short sentence she has already composed).
**Sample activities:**
- *Finger-space copy* (copy): copy a 3–4 word phrase she composed, using a finger (or a spacer) between words.
- *Just-right sizing:* lined or boxed paper; she copies a short phrase and checks that tall letters are tall and small letters are small.
- *Spacing fix-it:* the tutor shows a phrase written with no spaces; she rewrites it readably.
**Mastery gate:** writes a short phrase with clear word spaces and reasonably consistent sizing on most attempts, across ≥ 2 days.
**Stretch:** copy a two-sentence piece (one she composed) while keeping spacing — bridging toward R-WT-4.

### R-WT-3 · Capital at the start + end punctuation
**Can-do:** When writing by hand, she begins a sentence with a capital letter and ends it with the correct mark (. ? !), and uses a capital for "I" and for names she knows.
**Mode:** *handwritten*, applied to a single short sentence she has already composed on Ladder A.
**Sample activities:**
- *Capital-and-stop copy* (copy): she handwrites one of her own composed sentences, remembering the capital start and the end mark.
- *Mark the ending:* the tutor reads sentences with different tones; she picks and writes the right end mark (. ? !).
- *Fix-the-sentence:* a sentence written all lowercase with no period; she rewrites it correctly by hand.
**Mastery gate:** correctly applies a capital start and an appropriate end mark when handwriting a short composed sentence, on most attempts across ≥ 2 days.
**Stretch:** apply capitals and end marks across two handwritten sentences in a row.

### R-WT-4 · Copying stamina
**Can-do:** She can handwrite a short composed piece (two to three sentences) in one calm sitting, keeping formation, spacing, and basic punctuation — and knows it's fine to switch to typing or scribing for anything longer.
**Mode:** *handwritten*, gradually and only by her readiness; **the type/scribe option is always open** so length never becomes a fight.
**Sample activities:**
- *Publish a favorite by hand* (copy): she chooses one short piece she's proud of and handwrites it as the "published" copy, with a drawing.
- *A little longer each time:* the tutor nudges the handwritten amount up by a sentence only when the last one felt easy — stamina built in tiny, kind increments.
- *Author's choice:* she decides which parts of today's writing are handwritten and which are typed; both are honored equally.
**Mastery gate:** handwrites a 2–3 sentence composed piece in one sitting with legible formation, spacing, and basic punctuation, without frustration, across ≥ 2 days. (If stamina lags, this rung simply waits — Ladder A keeps moving regardless.)
**Stretch:** offer (never require) a slightly longer handwritten piece, or introduce simple cursive entry strokes if she's curious. Length beyond her comfort always returns to type/scribe.

## Placement probe

A light, playful "show me what you can do!" — not a test, and **never a handwriting test in disguise.** Place the two ladders **separately**; she will almost certainly land at very different rungs on each (that is the whole point).

**Composition probe (Ladder A) — done orally / scribed / typed, so her hand never caps the result:**
1. *Label it:* show a rich expedition picture (a volcano, a whale). "Tell me everything you see." Note single words → phrases (R-WC-1) vs. full spoken sentences (R-WC-2+).
2. *Say a sentence:* "Tell me one whole thing about this." Scribe it. Is it complete? Does she add detail unprompted (R-WC-3)?
3. *Tell me more:* "Tell me two or three things that go together." Listen for related sentences (R-WC-4) and beginning/middle/end (R-WC-5).
4. *Pick a genre:* offer story / facts / opinion. If she sustains a coherent 3-sentence piece, probe a short paragraph by keyboard or dictation (R-WC-6) and ask her to improve one word (R-WC-7).
   → **Place her at the highest composition rung she sustains independently, scribed or typed.** Expected entry per the README: she has big ideas, so she may place well above her hand — likely around R-WC-2/3, possibly higher when scribed.

**Transcription probe (Ladder B) — brief and separate, and stopped at the first sign of strain:**
1. *Write your name and a few letters you know.* Look at stroke direction and formation (R-WT-1).
2. *Copy a short phrase.* Check spacing and sizing (R-WT-2).
3. *Copy one short sentence.* Check capital start and end mark (R-WT-3); note comfort/stamina (R-WT-4).
   → **Place her on Ladder B wherever her hand actually is — expected near R-WT-1.** This number is *private scaffolding*, never a verdict on her as a writer.

**What placement must never do:** let a low Ladder-B result lower her Ladder-A rung. Her hand and her ideas are scored on different ladders, on purpose. Re-probe lightly if a strand looks mis-placed (composition too easy → jump her up; handwriting straining → step back a rung and shorten the dose).

## Skill tags

Dotted slugs for `skill.slug` (the rubric the tutor reads and the activities carry in `skillTags[]`). Composition tags live under `writing.compose.*`; transcription under `writing.transcription.*` — kept in **separate namespaces** so the agent can never let one gate the other.

**Composition (Ladder A):**
- `writing.compose.label` — label & caption: word → phrase *(R-WC-1)*
- `writing.compose.sentence` — one complete sentence, oral→scribed or framed *(R-WC-2)*
- `writing.compose.sentence-expand` — expand with detail / adjective / stronger verb *(R-WC-3)*
- `writing.compose.two-sentences` — two related sentences, with connectors *(R-WC-4)*
- `writing.compose.narrative` — 3-sentence narrative (beginning/middle/end) *(R-WC-5)*
- `writing.compose.informational` — 2–3 facts on a topic *(R-WC-5)*
- `writing.compose.opinion` — "I think ___ because ___" *(R-WC-5)*
- `writing.compose.paragraph` — topic sentence + supporting details *(R-WC-6)*
- `writing.compose.revise` — word choice, order, add detail *(R-WC-7)*

**Transcription (Ladder B):**
- `writing.transcription.letter-formation` — strokes by letter family *(R-WT-1)*
- `writing.transcription.spacing` — consistent spacing & sizing *(R-WT-2)*
- `writing.transcription.capitalization-punctuation` — capital start + end mark *(R-WT-3)*
- `writing.transcription.stamina` — copying stamina for short pieces *(R-WT-4)*

**Cross-cutting mode tags** (on activities, so the bridge is queryable — *which work was scribed/dictated/typed/handwritten*): `writing.mode.scribed`, `writing.mode.dictated`, `writing.mode.typed`, `writing.mode.handwritten`.
