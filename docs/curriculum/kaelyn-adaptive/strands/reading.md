# Reading & Comprehension: Mastery Ladder

**Strand of [Kaelyn's Adaptive Curriculum](../README.md)** · `domain: reading`
**Where Kaelyn starts:** reading early chapter books (Frog and Toad, Magic Tree House) with understanding.
**So this ladder teaches:** fluency on connected text, then comprehension that keeps getting deeper, across fiction **and** nonfiction. Decoding lives in the [Word Study](./word-study-vocabulary.md) strand; here the words are already hers, and the work is understanding them well and reading them like she means it.

This strand is taught the way the [README](../README.md) describes: **mastery, not weeks**, climbing one rung at a time, with skills practiced inside [knowledge expeditions](../README.md#knowledge-expeditions-the-glue) (volcanoes, Egypt, oceans, space, machines) because comprehension runs on background knowledge. Most rungs are taught on a real book she is reading or a real expedition text, then checked with a short, fresh, leveled activity. Almost every comprehension rung pairs with two app activity-types: a `reading-comprehension` interaction (the tutor asks a bounded, schema-validated question about a passage she just read and she picks or assembles the answer with evidence) and a `reading-retell` interaction (she reconstructs or summarizes what she read). The bedtime read-aloud, above her level, feeds every rung from the side: she meets harder ideas and words by ear long before she has to read them. We teach forward, we keep it warm, and we keep it real: a strong six-year-old should feel genuinely stretched here, never reviewed.

> **Where she enters:** placement (below) confirms it, but she almost certainly starts around **R-RD-1 / R-RD-2** and moves fast. A rung is a skill, not a grade level; she advances per rung independently, and the tutor offers the stretch the moment she is flying.

---

## R-RD-1: Fluency on early-chapter text

**She can…** read a passage from an early chapter book aloud smoothly: in phrases (not word-by-word), at a comfortable talking rate, with expression that fits the sentence, and she notices and fixes (self-corrects) when something does not sound right.

**Sample activities**
- **Phrase-scooping.** On a short Magic Tree House passage, she "scoops" the words into phrases with a finger or a pencil arc ("Jack looked up / and gasped"), then reads it again so it sounds like talking, not a list.
- **Read it like you mean it.** A line of dialogue from Frog and Toad read three ways (worried, excited, sleepy); she picks the one that fits the moment and reads it that way. Maps to a `reading-comprehension` item: "Toad says this right after he loses the button. Which voice fits?"
- **Punctuation drive.** A volcano expedition paragraph where periods are stop signs, commas are slow-downs, and the `!` after "It erupted!" gets the big voice. She reads to the marks.
- **Catch and fix.** She reads a short ocean passage aloud (recorded or to a grown-up); afterward she finds the one spot she stumbled and rereads it smoothly. Self-correction is the target, not a flawless first pass.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh, unpracticed early-chapter passages across **≥ 2 days**, she reads in phrases at a steady rate with fitting expression and self-corrects at least one slip, independently. (A grown-up listens; "smooth and makes sense" is the bar, not speed for its own sake.)

**Stretch → R-RD-2:** after a smooth read, she says in one breath *who* it was about and *what happened* (the doorway to retell).

## R-RD-2: Story elements & retell (beginning, middle, end)

**She can…** name the **characters**, **setting**, **problem**, and **solution** of a story she has read, and retell it in order (**beginning, middle, end**), keeping the important events and dropping the tiny ones.

**Sample activities**
- **Story-mountain.** After a Frog and Toad story, she fills a simple map: who / where / the problem / how it got solved. Maps to a `reading-retell` item with those four slots.
- **B-M-E in three cards.** She sorts (or draws) three cards (what happened first, next, last) for a Magic Tree House chapter, then retells from them without peeking at the book.
- **Whose problem is it?** In an Egypt-expedition story (a kid lost in a pyramid), she states the character's problem in one sentence and the solution in one sentence.
- **Too-long, just-right.** The tutor shows a 1-sentence retell (too thin) and a 10-sentence retell (everything, including "he blinked"); she picks the just-right middle and explains why. A `reading-comprehension` choose-and-justify item.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh stories across **≥ 2 days**, she names character / setting / problem / solution and gives an in-order B-M-E retell that keeps the key events, independently.

**Stretch → R-RD-3:** for one event she asks "*why* did he do that?", moving from *what happened* to *what it means*.

## R-RD-3: Inference (feelings, motivation, predictions, cause & effect)

**She can…** read between the lines of a story: tell how a character **feels** and **why they act** (even when the text does not say it outright), make a sensible **prediction** and back it up, explain **cause and effect** ("this happened *because*…"), and **draw a conclusion** from clues.

**Sample activities**
- **How do you know?** "Toad pulled the covers over his head and would not get up. How does he feel? What in the story tells you?" She names the feeling **and** the clue. A `reading-comprehension` item that requires the evidence, not just the guess.
- **What happens next?** Pause a Magic Tree House chapter at a cliffhanger; she predicts the next event and points to the clue that makes her think so. Read on and check: was the clue a good one?
- **Because-chains (volcanoes).** In a story set near an erupting volcano, she links cause to effect: "The villagers ran *because* the ground shook *because* the volcano woke up." Order the because-cards.
- **Detective conclusion.** A short space-expedition story never says the character is an astronaut; from three clues (a helmet, floating, no air) she concludes who they are and lists the clues.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh passages across **≥ 2 days**, she infers feeling/motivation, makes an evidence-backed prediction, **or** explains a cause-effect link, and in each case names the clue she used, independently.

**Stretch → R-RD-4:** asked "what was that whole part *mostly* about?", she answers with one idea instead of one detail.

## R-RD-4: Main idea & key details

**She can…** say what a passage (a paragraph, a section, a short chapter) is **mostly about** in one sentence, and pick out the **key details** that support it, telling the important from the merely interesting.

**Sample activities**
- **One-sentence "mostly about."** After an ocean-expedition section on how whales breathe, she gives the main idea in a single sentence, then lists two details that hold it up. A `reading-comprehension` main-idea item.
- **Detail or distractor?** Four facts from a machines passage; she keeps the two that support the main idea and tosses the two that are true but off-topic, and says why.
- **Title it.** She invents a title or heading for an untitled Egypt paragraph so that the title *is* the main idea (not just "Egypt").
- **Topic vs. main idea.** The tutor names the topic ("volcanoes") and asks for the main idea of *this* paragraph ("volcanoes can change the land around them"). She learns the difference. Choose-and-justify `reading-comprehension`.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh passages across **≥ 2 days**, she states a defensible main idea in one sentence and selects supporting key details (distinguishing them from off-topic facts), independently.

**Stretch → R-RD-5:** she starts using a passage's **heading, picture, or bold word** to find the main idea faster, stepping into nonfiction structure.

## R-RD-5: Nonfiction text features & finding text evidence

**She can…** use **nonfiction text features** (headings, captions, labels, diagrams, bold words, table of contents, glossary, index) to locate information, and **find and cite the text evidence** that answers a question ("It says right here…"), pointing to the exact words or picture.

**Sample activities**
- **Feature hunt (machines).** Given a nonfiction page about simple machines, she uses the heading to find the right section, the diagram labels to name a lever's parts, and the bold word to find its meaning in the glossary. A `reading-comprehension` "find it" task scored on using the feature, not guessing.
- **Prove it.** "How hot is lava?" She answers and then quotes or points to the exact sentence/caption in the volcano text that proves it. The citing is the skill. Maps to a `reading-comprehension` item that requires an evidence pointer.
- **Caption vs. body.** A space book where the photo caption and the paragraph each tell something different; she finds which one answers "How big is the Sun compared to Earth?"
- **Build the back matter.** For her expedition notebook she makes a tiny table of contents and a 4-word glossary (erupt, magma, vent, crater), learning the features by making them.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh nonfiction questions across **≥ 2 days**, she uses an appropriate text feature to locate the answer **and** cites the specific text/picture evidence, independently.

**Stretch → R-RD-6:** when she hits a word she does not know, she stops citing and starts *figuring it out from the sentence* (vocabulary-in-context).

## R-RD-6: Vocabulary in context while reading

**She can…** work out the meaning of an unfamiliar word **while reading**, using context clues in the sentence and nearby sentences (and pictures/glossary when nonfiction), then check whether her meaning makes sense by rereading.

> Word *structure* (prefixes, roots, suffixes) is taught in [Word Study](./word-study-vocabulary.md). Here the job is narrower and reading-specific: **don't stop the story, use the clues around the word.**

**Sample activities**
- **Clue-detective (oceans).** "The anglerfish uses a glowing *lure* to attract prey." She guesses *lure*, names the clue ("glowing… to attract"), then rereads to confirm. A `reading-comprehension` word-in-context item: pick the meaning + the clue.
- **Swap test.** She replaces the hard word with her guessed meaning and reads the sentence again: does it still make sense? ("…uses a glowing *bait* to attract prey." Yes.)
- **Clue or no clue?** Some sentences give a clue, some do not; she decides when to infer the meaning and when to use the glossary/ask. Knowing the limit is part of the skill.
- **Egypt word-banking.** While reading, she catches three "juicy" new words (*pharaoh, tomb, sacred*), records a context guess for each, and confirms one with the glossary for her expedition notebook.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh in-context words across **≥ 2 days**, she gives a sensible meaning, names the clue she used, and checks it by rereading, independently.

**Stretch → R-RD-7:** she notices *why the author* chose a word or wrote the piece at all (purpose and craft).

## R-RD-7: Author's purpose, comparing texts & theme

**She can…** tell **why an author wrote** a text (to inform, to entertain, to persuade/teach a lesson), **compare two texts** on the same topic or two versions of a story (what's alike, what's different), and name a simple **theme or lesson** in a story.

**Sample activities**
- **Why did they write it? (volcanoes).** A storybook about a dragon-volcano vs. a nonfiction page about magma: she says each author's purpose (entertain vs. inform) and how she can tell. A `reading-comprehension` purpose item.
- **Two-text Venn (oceans).** She compares two ocean texts (a poem about the sea and a facts page about tides): same topic, different purpose and information. She fills what's alike / different and says which she'd use to answer a fact question.
- **What's the lesson?** Across two Frog and Toad stories she names a shared theme ("good friends help each other") and points to a moment in each that shows it.
- **Same story, two tellings.** Two versions of an Egyptian myth; she finds one thing each version adds or changes and offers why an author might tell it that way.

**Mastery gate:** on **≥ 4 of 5** fresh tasks across **≥ 2 days**, she identifies author's purpose with a reason, compares two texts on a real point of likeness/difference, **or** states a defensible theme/lesson with support, independently.

**Stretch → R-RD-8:** she holds the thread across *many* pages, tracking and summarizing a whole longer book.

## R-RD-8: Sustaining & summarizing across a longer book

**She can…** read a longer book across several sittings and **keep the thread**: recall what happened earlier, track how a character changes, notice how chapters connect, and give a **tight summary** of multiple chapters (the arc, not every event) in her own words.

**Sample activities**
- **Chapter catch-up.** Before each new Magic Tree House chapter, she gives a two-sentence "previously…" so the story stays whole across days. A short `reading-retell` item per chapter.
- **Character-change tracker.** Across the book she logs how a character feels/acts at the start vs. the end ("brave by the end, scared at the start") and points to the turning point. A `reading-comprehension` across-text item.
- **Shrink-it summary.** After three chapters she writes/says a summary in five sentences or fewer (beginning, the big middle turn, where it stands now), keeping only what matters. (Composition load stays light: she can say it for a scribe or type it, per the [writing bridge](../README.md#4-the-writing-bridge-the-heart-of-this-design).)
- **Expedition book-talk.** Finishing a longer nonfiction expedition book (e.g., a kids' book on space), she gives a 60-second "book talk": what it's mostly about, two things she learned, one question she still has.

**Mastery gate:** across **≥ 2 days** and **≥ 2 different longer texts**, on **≥ 4 of 5** summary/recall tasks she accurately recalls prior events, tracks a change across chapters, and gives a tight multi-chapter summary in her own words, independently.

**Stretch → onward:** the tutor pulls in a genuinely harder book or a denser nonfiction expedition text and loops R-RD-3 through R-RD-8 on it at the new level. **This strand does not end:** it keeps handing her the next, harder, more interesting thing to understand.

## Placement probe

A quick, playful "show me how you read!", not a test. Aim for ~10 minutes; stop and place as soon as a rung clearly wobbles. Use a fresh early-chapter book she has not read (a new Magic Tree House or Frog and Toad) plus one short nonfiction expedition page (volcanoes or oceans).

1. **Read me a bit (R-RD-1).** She reads ~5–8 sentences aloud. Smooth, phrased, expressive, self-correcting? If word-by-word or flat, **start at R-RD-1**.
2. **Tell it back (R-RD-2).** "Who was it about? What happened (beginning, middle, end)?" In-order retell with the key parts? If retell is jumbled or detail-dumped, **start at R-RD-2**.
3. **Read between the lines (R-RD-3).** "How does she feel here, and how do you know? What do you think happens next?" Feeling + clue, evidence-backed prediction? If she states feelings but can't find the clue, **start at R-RD-3**.
4. **Mostly about (R-RD-4 / R-RD-5).** On the nonfiction page: "What's this part mostly about? Show me where it says how hot lava is." Main idea in a sentence + uses a heading/caption to locate and cite? If main idea is solid but she can't cite from text, **start at R-RD-5**; if she nails citing, probe vocabulary-in-context and **place at R-RD-6+**.
5. **Stretch peek (R-RD-7).** "Why did the author write this one?" / "What's the lesson?" If she handles purpose/theme with ease, place high (**R-RD-7**) and try a multi-chapter recall to check **R-RD-8**.

**Placement rule (per the README):** too easy at a rung → jump her up; a clear wobble → that rung is her entry. Re-probe lightly any time her results say she's mis-placed.
