# Word Study & Vocabulary

**Strand of [Kaelyn's Adaptive Curriculum](../README.md) (Program 02)** · A mastery ladder
**Where Kaelyn actually is:** past CVC and sight words, reading early chapter books with understanding
**What this strand teaches:** multisyllable decoding, **morphology** (prefixes, suffixes, base words, Greek and Latin roots), and **vocabulary depth** (shades of meaning, multiple meanings, context clues, Tier-2 academic words)

> The old "vocab" was sight words plus CVC. For a child who reads *Magic Tree House*, that is review, not learning. This strand is the real engine of a large vocabulary and strong spelling. It teaches her to **unlock big, unfamiliar words on her own** by hearing their syllables, seeing their parts, and reasoning out their meaning. We confirm single-syllable phonics is solid in about ten minutes (placement probe below), then we climb.

---

## How this strand is taught

Five habits run through every rung. They are the method; the rungs are just the order.

- **Explicit, then applied.** We name the pattern or word-part out loud and show how it works ("a closed syllable ends in a consonant, so the vowel is short"). Then, the same day, we hunt it in her real reading and use it in her real writing. A skill she cannot find in *Frog and Toad* is not learned yet.
- **Multisensory.** She builds, moves, and sorts: tiles and cards for morphemes, tapping and looping syllables, sticky notes she physically rearranges. Hands first, worksheet never.
- **Build-and-sort.** Two engines do most of the work. **Build:** assemble a word from parts (`re + view`, `un + happi + ness`) and read what you made. **Sort:** put a stack of words into the right buckets (long vs short *a*, open vs closed syllable, *-ed* that says /t/ vs /d/ vs /ed/), then say the rule the sort just taught.
- **Decoding and meaning together.** Reading a big word and knowing what it means are one job here, not two. Every multisyllable and morphology rung asks both "what does it say?" and "what does it mean?"
- **Spiraled, never abandoned.** Old patterns keep showing up inside new words. When she learns roots, the syllable types she already owns are how she chops the root word apart. Mastery means it still works weeks later, in the wild.

**How it maps to the app.** Activities are leveled and skill-tagged (see the master design, §2 and §7). Most word-work rungs use an extended **`phonics-wordbuild`** (tiles become morphemes and syllables, not just letters), a **sort** interaction (drag words into rule-buckets), a **word-from-parts** interaction (combine prefix, root, and suffix, then read and define the result), and a **context-clue** interaction (a sentence with one unknown word and a "what does it mean, and how do you know?" prompt). The agentic tutor generates fresh items at her exact rung and pulls vocabulary from her current expedition (*Volcanoes*, *Ancient Egypt*, *Oceans & Whales*, *Space*), so new words hang on real knowledge and stick.

**Mastery gate (strand default).** A rung is **solid** when she works **independently** and gets **≥ 4 of 5 fresh items right across ≥ 2 different days** (retention, not one lucky session). Decoding rungs add a fluency floor: she reads the target words **accurately and without finger-by-finger sounding out**. Meaning rungs add a use floor: she can **use the word or strategy in her own sentence**, not just recognize it. Below the gate she is **emerging** and gets more varied practice. Every rung has a **stretch** that points at the next rung; if she grabs it, she advances early.

## The rungs, in order

The ladder climbs in four moves: **(A) advanced decoding** at the multisyllable level (R-WS-1 to R-WS-5); **(B) spelling generalizations** that make her a confident speller and a faster decoder (R-WS-6); **(C) morphology**, the heart of the strand, where she learns to build and break words from meaningful parts (R-WS-7 to R-WS-11); and **(D) vocabulary depth**, where she works with meaning itself (R-WS-12 to R-WS-15).

---

### R-WS-1 · Vowel teams at the multisyllable level
**Can-do:** "I read longer words with vowel teams (*ai, ay, ee, ea, igh, oa, ow, oo*), even when the team is buried in the middle of a two-syllable word."

She likely owns vowel teams in single-syllable words (*rain, feet, boat*). This rung moves them up a floor, into *railway, freedom, rainbow, oatmeal, daylight, raincoat*, where she has to spot the team inside a bigger word and keep its sound steady across syllables.

**Sample activities**
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (vowel-team tiles):** the tile set includes whole teams (`ai`, `ee`, `igh`, `oa`) as single pieces. Build *raindrop, seaweed, highway, toadstool*; read each aloud. Expedition flavor: *Oceans* gives *seaweed, seacoast, daylight* on the reef.
- **Team sort:** a stack of words sorts by which long sound the team spells (*long a: rain, play, sail* vs *long e: feet, leaf, seal*). Then she says the rule: "two vowels walking, the first one usually talks."
- **Buried-team hunt:** in a real page (*Magic Tree House*), she highlights every vowel team she can find and reads those words back. Decoding applied immediately.

**Mastery gate:** reads ≥ 4/5 fresh two-syllable vowel-team words accurately, on ≥ 2 days, without sounding out letter by letter.
**Stretch:** *igh* and *ough* oddballs (*delight, thoughtful*) and three-syllable team words (*understanding* splits to *un-der-stand*).

### R-WS-2 · R-controlled vowels in longer words
**Can-do:** "I read words where a vowel is changed by a following *r* (*ar, or, er, ir, ur*), including in multisyllable words like *garden, forest, perfect, thirsty, turtle*."

Bossy *r* makes the vowel say something new. The work here is holding that sound steady inside a bigger word, and noticing that *er, ir, ur* often share one sound (*her, bird, fur*).

**Sample activities**
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (r-controlled tiles):** `ar`, `or`, `er`, `ir`, `ur` are single tiles. Build *farmer, morning, garden, summer, thirsty*. Expedition flavor: *Ancient Egypt* gives *desert, marker, mortar*.
- **Three-bucket sort:** sort words by sound, not spelling. One bucket for *ar (car)*, one for *or (corn)*, and one for the shared *er/ir/ur (her, bird, fur)* family. The sort teaches that three spellings can make one sound.
- **Reading sweep:** read a short nonfiction paragraph from the expedition and circle every bossy-*r* word, then reread it smoothly.

**Mastery gate:** reads ≥ 4/5 fresh multisyllable r-controlled words accurately across ≥ 2 days.
**Stretch:** *air/are/ear* (*chair, bear, pear*) and *or*-after-*w* saying /er/ (*work, word, world*).

### R-WS-3 · Diphthongs and other vowel patterns in longer words
**Can-do:** "I read gliding-vowel words (*oi, oy, ou, ow*) and other vowel patterns (*aw, au, oo*) inside longer words like *poison, enjoyment, mountain, powerful, awesome, autumn*."

Diphthongs glide from one vowel sound to another. She has met them in *coin* and *cow*; now they live inside *poison, mountain, powerful, enjoyment*.

**Sample activities**
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (diphthong tiles):** build *cowboy, mountain, poison, awesome, autumn*; read aloud. Expedition flavor: *Volcanoes* gives *mountain, powder, outburst*.
- **Sound-glide sort:** sort by which glide the spelling makes. Put *oi/oy (coin, boy)* together and *ou/ow (cloud, cow)* together, and notice the spelling often depends on where the sound sits in the word (*oy* and *ow* like the end).
- **Match-the-meaning build:** build the diphthong word, then point to a picture or say what it means (*mountain* = a very tall landform), tying decode to meaning.

**Mastery gate:** reads ≥ 4/5 fresh multisyllable diphthong/vowel-pattern words across ≥ 2 days.
**Stretch:** schwa-flavored versions where the glide softens in an unstressed syllable (*mountain* sounds like "moun-tn"; *poison* like "poi-zn").

### R-WS-4 · The six syllable types
**Can-do:** "I can name and read the six syllable types (**closed, open, silent-e (VCe), vowel team, r-controlled, and consonant-le**), and I use the type to know whether a vowel is short or long."

This is the keystone rung of the decoding half. The six types are a complete toolkit: every English syllable is one of them, and the type tells you the vowel sound. Closed (*cat*, short) vs open (*go*, long) is the big one; consonant-*le* (*-ble, -tle, -dle* as in *table, little, candle*) is the one most kids miss.

**Sample activities**
- **Six-house sort:** six labeled "houses," one per type. She sorts single syllables (*cat, go, cake, rain, car, ble*) into the right house and says why ("*go* is open, so the *o* is long"). This is the multisensory core of the rung.
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (type-aware):** build a syllable and flip a card to label its type; the app confirms the vowel sound. Contrast pairs make it click: *hop* (closed, short *o*) vs *ho-* in *hope* (silent-e, long *o*); *cub* vs *cube*.
- **Type-spotting in reading:** in a chapter-book sentence, she taps a chosen word and labels each syllable's type out loud before reading it smoothly.

**Mastery gate:** correctly names the type and predicts the vowel sound for ≥ 4/5 fresh syllables across ≥ 2 days, including at least one open and one consonant-*le*.
**Stretch:** the "open or closed?" judgment calls where a single consonant could go either way (*robot* splits to *ro-bot*, open; *robin* splits to *rob-in*, closed), a preview of R-WS-5's splitting rules.

### R-WS-5 · Dividing and decoding multisyllable words
**Can-do:** "I split an unfamiliar long word into syllables myself and read it, using patterns like **VC/CV** (*rab-bit, nap-kin*), **V/CV and VC/V** (*ti-ger* vs *cab-in*), and **C+le** (*ta-ble*), then I blend the parts back together."

Now she runs the toolkit. She meets a word she has never seen, **chops it where the rules say**, reads each chunk using its syllable type, and blends. This is the single most powerful decoding move on the whole ladder: it is how she reads *fantastic, important, volcanic, adventure* without being told.

**Sample activities**
- **Chop-it routine (the core loop):** spot the vowels, draw the split, label each part's type, read each part, blend. Practiced on expedition words: *vol-can-ic, pyr-a-mid, con-ti-nent, ad-ven-ture*. Make it physical: she draws the slash with a finger or a dry-erase marker.
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (syllable tiles):** the app hands her the syllables of a long word scrambled (*ven · ad · ture*); she orders and reads them, then the word reassembles. Build-and-read for decoding.
- **VC/CV vs V/CV sort:** sort two-syllable words by where the split lands and which vowel sound results (*rab-bit* short vs *ti-ger* long), then state the rule.
- **Cold-read challenge:** the tutor shows three never-seen multisyllable expedition words; she chops and reads each with no help. This is the rung's real-world test.

**Mastery gate:** independently divides and reads ≥ 4/5 fresh 2–3 syllable words across ≥ 2 days, blending to a smooth whole word.
**Stretch:** 4+ syllable words (*in-vis-i-ble, ar-che-ol-o-gy*) and using the split plus context to also say what the word means, the bridge into morphology and vocabulary depth.

### R-WS-6 · Spelling generalizations (doubling, drop-e, y→i)
**Can-do:** "I know the spelling rules for adding endings (**double the final consonant** in *hop → hopping*, **drop the silent e** in *bake → baking*, and **change y to i** in *happy → happier*), and I use them when I spell."

Decoding has been the engine so far; this rung turns the same machinery toward **spelling**, and it pays back into decoding because she now understands *why* a word looks the way it does. Three generalizations cover most of it: 1-1-1 doubling, drop-*e*, and *y* to *i*.

**Sample activities**
- **Add-the-ending build:** start word plus an ending tile (*-ing, -ed, -er, -est*). She applies the rule and spells the result: *run + ing → running* (double), *hope + ed → hoped* (drop-*e*), *easy + er → easier* (*y* to *i*). The app checks the spelling.
- **Three-rule sort:** sort finished words by which rule built them (*swimming, hoping, prettier*) and say the rule each column proves.
- **Spelling-in-writing tie-in:** when she composes (see the [writing strand](./writing-composition.md)), she fixes one ending word using a rule and names the rule. Real application, low stakes.
- **Rule-or-not check:** a quick "does this word double?" game, contrasting *jump + ing* (no, two final consonants) with *hop + ing* (yes), sharpening the 1-1-1 condition.

**Mastery gate:** correctly spells ≥ 4/5 fresh ending-words across ≥ 2 days using all three rules; can state which rule applies.
**Stretch:** the trickier conditions: *y* to *i* but *not* before *-ing* (*crying*, not *criing*), and doubling only when the last syllable is stressed (*begin → beginning* vs *visit → visiting*).

### R-WS-7 · Inflectional suffixes (-s, -ed, -ing)
**Can-do:** "I read and understand the inflected endings *-s/-es*, *-ed*, and *-ing*, I know they change a word's number or tense without changing its base meaning, and I hear the three sounds of *-ed* (/t/, /d/, /ed/)."

This is the gateway to morphology: the first time a word-part carries **meaning** (more than one; happened in the past; happening now). The base word stays visible (*jump → jumped*), which trains her eye to see a base inside a bigger word, the exact skill the rest of the strand needs.

**Sample activities**
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (morpheme mode):** base-word tile plus ending tile. Build *walk + ed, fox + es, run + ning*; read it and say what changed ("*walked* means it already happened").
- **The three sounds of -ed sort:** sort *-ed* words by the sound the ending makes: /t/ (*jumped*), /d/ (*played*), /ed/ (*wanted*). A favorite "did you notice?" discovery: the ending is spelled the same but said three ways.
- **Base + ending, two columns:** given *jumped*, she writes the base (*jump*) and the ending (*-ed*) in two columns and tells what the ending did. Breaking words apart, the inverse of building.
- **In-text hunt:** in her chapter book, find five *-ing* or *-ed* words, name each base, and tell whether it is past or happening-now.

**Mastery gate:** reads and explains ≥ 4/5 fresh inflected words across ≥ 2 days, naming the base and what the ending does; sorts the three *-ed* sounds.
**Stretch:** inflected endings stacked on spelling rules from R-WS-6 (*hurrying, biggest, copied*) and irregular contrasts (*ran* has no *-ed*, a "why?" worth asking).

### R-WS-8 · Derivational suffixes for comparison and adverbs (-er, -est, -ly)
**Can-do:** "I read and understand suffixes that change how a word is used: *-er* and *-est* to compare (*fast → faster → fastest*), and *-ly* to make an adverb (*quick → quickly*), and I notice the word's job in a sentence changes."

Where inflectional endings tweaked number and tense, **derivational** suffixes start changing a word's *job* and shade of meaning. *-er/-est* build the comparison ladder (a perfect place to teach degree); *-ly* turns a describing word into a how-word.

**Sample activities**
- **Comparison-ladder build:** stack *fast → faster → fastest* with tiles and order them on a little ladder, matching to a picture (one cheetah, two, three). Ties straight into vocabulary shades of meaning later.
- **Make-it-an-adverb word-from-parts:** *quiet + ly → quietly*; then she uses it in a sentence about the expedition ("The explorer moved *quietly* through the tomb").
- **Suffix sort:** sort words by suffix job, comparing (*-er/-est*) vs how (*-ly*), and say what each suffix does.
- **In-writing application:** in a composed sentence, upgrade a plain word with *-ly* or use *-est* to make a comparison.

**Mastery gate:** reads, explains, and uses ≥ 4/5 fresh *-er/-est/-ly* words across ≥ 2 days, including using one correctly in her own sentence.
**Stretch:** the *-er* that means "a person who does" (*teach → teacher*, *farm → farmer*), a different *-er* from "more," a great noticing that previews R-WS-9.

### R-WS-9 · Derivational suffixes that shift meaning (-ful, -less, -ness, -er "one who")
**Can-do:** "I read and understand suffixes that change a word's **meaning and type**: *-ful* (full of), *-less* (without), *-ness* (the state of being), and the *-er/-or* that means 'one who' (*sail → sailor*), and I use the suffix to figure out the word."

This rung makes suffixes a **meaning tool**, not just an ending. *-ful* and *-less* are a clean opposite pair (*hopeful / hopeless*); *-ness* turns a quality into a thing (*kind → kindness*); *-er/-or* names a doer. She is now reasoning about meaning from a part, the core promise of this strand.

**Sample activities**
- **Opposite-pair build:** build *hope + ful* and *hope + less* side by side, read both, and explain how one little part flips the meaning. Expedition flavor: a *fear-less* explorer, a *power-ful* volcano, a *harm-less* creature.
- **Word-from-parts with a meaning check:** combine base plus suffix, read the word, **and** define it from its parts ("*kindness* = the state of being kind"). The app accepts a spoken or chosen definition.
- **Suffix-meaning sort:** sort words under their suffix's meaning (*full of / without / state of being / one who*), using the suffix to place each word.
- **Add-a-suffix in writing:** describe an expedition character with a *-ful* or *-less* word and justify it ("The pharaoh was *powerful* because…").

**Mastery gate:** reads, defines from parts, and uses ≥ 4/5 fresh suffixed words across ≥ 2 days, including *-ful/-less* opposites and one *-ness* word.
**Stretch:** stacking two suffixes (*hope-ful-ly, care-less-ness*) and spotting a base inside a longer word despite a spelling change (*happy → happiness*).

### R-WS-10 · Prefixes (un-, re-, pre-, dis-, mis-, non-)
**Can-do:** "I read words with common prefixes and use the prefix's meaning to understand the word: *un-/dis-/non-* (not), *re-* (again), *pre-* (before), *mis-* (wrong), as in *unhappy, redo, preview, disagree, misread, nonstop*."

Prefixes attach to the **front** and reliably change meaning. Six high-frequency prefixes unlock an enormous number of words, and they are wonderfully predictable: if she knows *re-* means "again," she can reason out *reread, rebuild, rewrite, return* the first time she meets them.

**Sample activities**
- **`phonics-wordbuild` (prefix + base):** prefix tiles snap onto base words. Build *un + happy, re + play, pre + heat, dis + appear, mis + spell, non + stop*; read each and say the new meaning.
- **Prefix-meaning word-from-parts:** show a base (*do*), add a prefix (*re-*), get *redo*, and she states the meaning ("do it again"). Then swap the prefix (*un-* gives *undo*) to feel the meaning move.
- **Prefix sort by meaning:** sort prefixed words under *not / again / before / wrong*, using the prefix to place each.
- **Unlock-the-word challenge:** the tutor shows a never-seen prefixed expedition word (*reactivate, preheat, nonliving*); she covers the prefix, reads the base, then adds the prefix's meaning to infer the whole. This is the rung's payoff.

**Mastery gate:** reads, defines from the prefix, and uses ≥ 4/5 fresh prefixed words across ≥ 2 days, covering at least *un-*, *re-*, and one of *pre-/dis-/mis-/non-*.
**Stretch:** prefix plus base plus suffix all at once (*un-help-ful, re-build-ing, dis-respect-ful*) and noticing when *un-* attaches to a verb to reverse it (*untie, unlock*) vs an adjective to negate it (*unkind*).

### R-WS-11 · Base words plus Greek and Latin roots
**Can-do:** "I know that many big words are built on **Greek and Latin roots** that carry meaning (*tele* far, *port* carry, *struct* build, *bio* life, *geo* earth, *graph* write/draw, *meter* measure), and I combine parts to read and infer the meaning of words I have never seen, like *telegraph, transport, construct, biography, geography, thermometer*."

This is the summit of the morphology climb and the real engine of an advanced vocabulary. Roots are **meaning atoms** that recombine endlessly. A child who knows *tele* = far, *graph* = write, and *meter* = measure can attack *telegraph, telephone, photograph, thermometer, geography* on sight, and that is exactly the "unlock big words" power the parent asked for. Roots also connect straight to her expeditions: *Space* lives on *astro/geo/tele*, *Oceans* on *bio/hydro*, *How Machines Work* on *struct/port/meter*.

**Sample activities**
- **Root-meaning anchor cards:** each root gets a card with its meaning and a picture-anchor (*tele* = far, drawn as a telescope). She collects them like a deck; mastered roots earn a badge (see master design §6).
- **Word-from-parts, root edition (the core activity):** combine a root with another root or affix and read **and** infer the meaning: *tele + graph* is "write from far away"; *geo + graph + y* is "writing about the earth"; *thermo + meter* is "measures heat." The app accepts her reasoned definition.
- **Root web / sort:** put a root in the center (*port* = carry) and branch to its family (*transport, export, portable, import*); say how "carry" lives in each. Or sort a stack of words by their shared root.
- **Infer-the-unknown challenge:** the tutor presents a genuinely new word built from known roots (*biography, geology, telescope*) in an expedition sentence; she breaks it into roots, assembles the meaning, then checks it against the sentence. Decoding plus meaning plus context, all at once, the whole strand in one move.

**Mastery gate:** for ≥ 4/5 fresh root-built words across ≥ 2 days, identifies the roots, reads the word, and gives a reasonable parts-based meaning; knows at least 6 roots cold.
**Stretch:** words built from **two** roots she must combine (*photo + graph, micro + scope, tele + vision*), and using a known root to make a smart guess at a brand-new word *before* checking, then confirming with a dictionary or the sentence.

### R-WS-12 · Synonyms, antonyms, and shades of meaning
**Can-do:** "I give synonyms and antonyms for a word, and I can order words by **degree** (*happy → glad → thrilled*, or *cool → cold → freezing*), choosing the word that fits how strong I mean."

Now the strand turns fully to **meaning**. Vocabulary depth is not just knowing more words; it is knowing *which* word, and how strong. Ordering near-synonyms by intensity ("shades of meaning") is the move that makes her word choice precise and her reading sensitive to an author's exact wording.

**Sample activities**
- **Shades-of-meaning paint strip:** a paint-chip strip from pale to deep; she places *happy / glad / pleased / thrilled / ecstatic* light-to-strong and reads the gradient. The signature multisensory activity for this rung.
- **Synonym/antonym sort or match:** match a word to its synonym and to its antonym (*enormous* pairs with *gigantic* and opposes *tiny*), or sort a pile into "means the same / means the opposite."
- **Pick-the-strongest-word in writing:** revising a composed sentence, she upgrades a flat word ("The volcano was *big*") to the precise one ("The volcano was *enormous*") and says why it is better. Ties to the [writing strand's](./writing-composition.md) "pick the stronger word" revision.
- **Degree challenge:** the tutor gives three near-synonyms scrambled (*warm, scorching, hot*); she orders them weak-to-strong.

**Mastery gate:** for ≥ 4/5 fresh words across ≥ 2 days, gives a synonym and an antonym, and correctly orders a 3-word intensity set; uses a precise word in her own sentence.
**Stretch:** longer gradients (5+ words) and noticing **connotation**, the way synonyms can carry a feeling (*thin* vs *skinny* vs *slender*), so the "right" word depends on what you mean.

### R-WS-13 · Multiple-meaning words
**Can-do:** "I know that one word can have several meanings, and I use the sentence to tell which meaning is in play, like *bark* (a dog's sound, or a tree's covering) or *bright* (full of light, or very smart)."

Chapter books are full of words doing double duty. A reader who locks onto the first meaning of *trunk* (a suitcase) will be lost when the elephant's *trunk* shows up. This rung makes her flexible and alert to context, and it sets up context clues next.

**Sample activities**
- **Two-meanings map:** a word in the center (*bat*), two pictures or short definitions branching out (the animal, the baseball bat); she gives a sentence for each meaning.
- **Which-meaning context interaction:** the app shows the same word in two expedition sentences ("The ship had a tall *trunk*" vs "The elephant raised its *trunk*"); she picks the right meaning for each and says the clue word that told her.
- **Pun/joke spotting:** read-aloud jokes that hinge on a double meaning ("Why did the *bark* fall off the tree? It got *board*!"); naming why it is funny proves she sees both meanings. Delightful and rigorous.

**Mastery gate:** for ≥ 4/5 fresh multiple-meaning words across ≥ 2 days, supplies two correct meanings and chooses the right one for a given sentence, citing the clue.
**Stretch:** words with **3+** meanings (*run, set, point*) and homophones that sound alike but split by spelling and meaning (*their / there / they're*, *to / too / two*).

### R-WS-14 · Context clues
**Can-do:** "When I meet an unfamiliar word in my reading, I use clues in the surrounding sentences (a **definition, an example, a synonym, an antonym, or the general sense**) to figure out what it probably means, then I check if my guess makes sense."

This rung fuses everything below it. When she hits an unknown word, she now has two tools working together: **break it by its parts** (R-WS-10, R-WS-11) and **read the clues around it** (here). That combination is exactly how strong readers grow vocabulary by the thousands of words a year, without a dictionary at their elbow.

**Sample activities**
- **Context-clue interaction (the core activity):** an expedition sentence with one unknown word and a "What does it probably mean? What clue told you?" prompt. Clue types rotate: definition ("a *caldera*, **a giant crater left when a volcano collapses**, formed when…"), example, synonym, antonym. She infers, then names the clue type.
- **Two-tool unlock:** a deliberately hard word she can attack **both** ways, by parts *and* by context (*"the soil was so* infertile *that nothing would grow"*: *in-* = not, *fertil* = able to grow, plus the "nothing would grow" clue). She uses both and checks they agree.
- **Predict-then-check:** she guesses a word's meaning from context, then the tutor reveals the real definition; she compares. Builds the habit of checking, not just guessing.
- **In-her-reading capture:** when a real unknown word appears in her chapter book, she runs the routine out loud with the parent: parts? clues? best guess? The strand applied to actual reading.

**Mastery gate:** for ≥ 4/5 fresh in-context unknown words across ≥ 2 days, infers a reasonable meaning and names the clue (or word-part) she used.
**Stretch:** passages where the clue is **further away** (a sentence or two later) or where she must combine context with a Greek/Latin root to pin a precise meaning.

### R-WS-15 · Tier-2 academic vocabulary
**Can-do:** "I learn, understand, and **use** high-value 'Tier-2' words that show up across many books and subjects, words like *observe, predict, compare, describe, demonstrate, eventually, fortunate, reluctant, essential, sufficient*, including in my own talking and writing."

Tier-2 words are the words of educated language: not everyday words (*dog, run*) and not narrow technical terms (*magma, isotope*), but the cross-topic, high-mileage words that carry meaning across every subject and every book she will ever read. Owning them deeply, used and not just recognized, is what a big vocabulary actually *is*, and it lifts her comprehension and her writing at the same time. This rung is ongoing: it never "finishes," it keeps generating her next words.

**Sample activities**
- **Word of the day, done richly:** a Tier-2 word from the current expedition, taught with a kid-friendly meaning, an example and a non-example, and a quick "use it about today's reading." Anchored in real context, not a list.
- **Four-square word card:** word / meaning in her words / a picture or example / her own sentence. A keeper card; mastered words fill a personal word wall.
- **Use-it-or-lose-it tally:** she earns a mark each time she *uses* a target word correctly in conversation or writing during the day, rewarding **use** (the real gate) rather than recognition.
- **Tier-2 in revision:** swap a plain phrase in her writing for a precise Tier-2 word ("then" becomes "*eventually*"; "important" becomes "*essential*") and explain the upgrade. Connects to the [reading](./reading.md) and [writing](./writing-composition.md) strands.

**Mastery gate:** for a target set, **uses** ≥ 4/5 words correctly in her own speech or writing across ≥ 2 days (use, not just definition-matching) and explains each meaning. Because the rung is ongoing, "mastery" applies per word set; the tutor keeps queuing new sets.
**Stretch:** word **families and morphology together** (*observe → observer → observation → observant*; *predict → prediction → predictable*) and using a precise Tier-2 word unprompted in a composed paragraph, the moment vocabulary depth shows up as real authorship.

## Placement probe

A quick, playful "show me what you can do!", about **10 to 15 minutes**, no pressure, no score read aloud. The job is two-part: **confirm single-syllable phonics is solid** (we expect it is, per the master design, so this is fast), then **find her real entry rung** in multisyllable decoding and morphology. Stop a line of questioning the moment it gets hard; that is her edge, and the rung just below it is where she starts.

**Step 1: confirm the floor (single-syllable phonics).** Have her read about 8 single-syllable words spanning short vowels, digraphs, blends, silent-e, and a vowel team: *cat, ship, frog, stamp, cake, rain, bird, cloud*. Smooth and accurate? The floor is solid; do **not** dwell here, move on. (If two or more wobble, note which pattern and start her in the [reading](./reading.md) decodable work in parallel; this strand still begins at R-WS-1, the multisyllable level.)

**Step 2: multisyllable decoding (R-WS-1 to R-WS-5).** Have her read, in order, until it gets hard:
- a two-syllable vowel-team word, *rainbow* (R-WS-1)
- an r-controlled multisyllable word, *garden* (R-WS-2)
- a diphthong multisyllable word, *mountain* (R-WS-3)
- name the type of a syllable, or read a consonant-*le* word, *table* (R-WS-4)
- chop and read a never-seen long word, *fantastic* (R-WS-5)

Her entry rung is the first one she **cannot** do smoothly and independently.

**Step 3: morphology (R-WS-7 to R-WS-11), only if she cleared Step 2.** Probe forward:
- read and explain an inflected word, *jumped* ("what does *-ed* tell you?") (R-WS-7)
- read and use a suffixed word, *hopeless* ("what does it mean?") (R-WS-9)
- read a prefixed word and give its meaning, *redo, unhappy* (R-WS-10)
- the unlock question: show *telegraph* or *transport* and ask, "Can you break this into parts? What might it mean?" (R-WS-11)

**Step 4: word depth (R-WS-12 to R-WS-15), if morphology is strong.** Quick touches: "Tell me a word that means almost the same as *happy*, and an even stronger one." (shades, R-WS-12) · "*Bark*: can you think of two meanings?" (R-WS-13) · read a sentence with an unknown word and ask, "what do you think it means, and how do you know?" (context, R-WS-14).

**Placing her.** Start at the first rung where she is *not yet* independent; the rung just below is review and gets only a light confirming pass. Given the master design's expectation, she most likely lands around **R-WS-4/R-WS-5 (syllable types and splitting), moving quickly into morphology**, with the decoding rungs below confirmed fast. Re-probe lightly whenever results suggest a mis-place: too easy, jump her up; struggling, step back one rung (master design §2).
